**Why This Interpretation Matters**

The conventional narrative—blaming speculation, inequality, or lack of regulation—mistakes symptoms for causes. The speculative excesses of the 1920s were *enabled* by the monetary disorder from WWI. The inequality resulted from inflation redistributing wealth to asset owners. The regulatory failures occurred because regulators didn't understand the new monetary reality.

The Depression's global synchronization proves this was a monetary phenomenon. Countries that abandoned gold early (Britain in 1931) recovered quickly. Those that clung to it (France, the U.S. until 1933) suffered prolonged agony. The entire catastrophe stemmed from attempting to reconstruct a pre-war monetary order atop a foundation of war-created fiat money and debt.

The proof? World War II saw even greater physical destruction and government spending, yet no comparable post-war depression. Why? Because there was no attempt to return to pre-war monetary arrangements. The Bretton Woods system acknowledged the new reality of managed currencies—a lesson learned from WWI's monetary time bomb that exploded in 1929.

The Great Depression wasn't a failure of capitalism—it was the inevitable consequence of governments discovering they could print prosperity during wartime, then refusing to accept the hangover when peace returned.
