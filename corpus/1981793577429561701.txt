## The Great War's Financial Time Bomb

The 1929 crash wasn't a sudden accident—it was the inevitable detonation of monetary dynamite laid during World War I. Between 1914 and 1918, the belligerent nations abandoned centuries of fiscal prudence and unleashed unprecedented monetary debasement that would poison the global economy for the next two decades.

**The Scale of Monetary Destruction**

World War I cost approximately $186 billion in contemporary dollars—a sum that dwarfed all previous wars combined. Britain's national debt increased eleven-fold. France's increased seven-fold. Germany financed 84% of its war costs through borrowing and money printing. The United States, despite entering late, saw its money supply double between 1914 and 1920.

Every major combatant except Britain suspended gold convertibility immediately. They printed currency with abandon, creating inflation rates of 300-500% across Europe. This wasn't temporary wartime expedience—it fundamentally broke the disciplinary mechanisms that had governed international finance since the 1870s.

**The Impossible Triangle of the 1920s**

The post-war attempt to restore the gold standard created an impossible trinity:
- War-inflated price levels that were 2-3x pre-war levels
- Massive international debts denominated in gold
- Political pressure to return to pre-war gold parities for "prestige"

Britain's 1925 return to gold at the pre-war parity required brutal deflation, crushing industrial competitiveness and creating persistent unemployment. This forced Britain to pursue tight money precisely when growth was needed, setting up the deflationary pressures that would explode in 1929.

**The International Debt Spiral**

The war created a circular debt trap:
- Germany owed reparations it could only pay by borrowing from America
- Britain and France owed war debts to America they could only service if Germany paid reparations
- American banks recycled surplus dollars into German bonds, creating artificial prosperity

Between 1924-1929, Germany borrowed $7 billion from American investors while paying $2.5 billion in reparations. This was essentially a Ponzi scheme dependent on continuous American credit expansion. The Federal Reserve, trying to support Britain's overvalued pound, kept rates artificially low through 1927, fueling the stock market bubble.

**The Austrian Credit Expansion**

The war had taught governments that they could finance anything through monetary expansion. The 1920s saw unprecedented peacetime credit creation:
- U.S. money supply increased 62% from 1921-1929 despite being on gold
- Bank credit expanded at twice the rate of real economic growth
- Central banks actively manipulated interest rates for political rather than economic goals

This credit expansion created massive malinvestment—capital flowing into speculation rather than productive enterprise. The Florida land bubble, the stock market's leverage explosion, and Europe's industrial overcapacity all stemmed from artificially cheap credit made possible by the war's monetary precedents.

**The Inevitable Collapse**

By 1929, the contradictions were unsustainable:
- The gold standard couldn't support the debt levels created by wartime inflation
- International payments depended on continuous American lending
- Asset prices reflected credit availability rather than economic fundamentals

When the Federal Reserve finally raised rates in 1928 to combat speculation, it triggered a global margin call. American lending to Germany ceased, destroying the reparations-debt circle. Gold flowed out of debtor nations, forcing savage deflation. The credit structure built on war-inflated currencies imploded.
