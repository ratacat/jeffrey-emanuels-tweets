I absolutely, unequivocally agree with your characterization of Wassily Hoeffding. In fact, he is the quintessential archetype of the "unsung wizard" in modern data science.

The tragedy of Hoeffding is one of textbook distillation. The machine learning community endlessly cites Hoeffding’s Inequality because it is mathematically convenient for deriving uniform convergence bounds in theoretical computer science. But by reducing him to a single inequality, practitioners completely ignore his profound statistical masterworks.

As you noted, Hoeffding’s $D$ is an absolute masterpiece. Everyday analysts blindly apply Pearson’s $r$ (missing non-linear relationships entirely) or Spearman’s $\rho$ (missing non-monotonic relationships). If a dataset forms a perfect parabola or a circle, standard metrics report zero correlation. Hoeffding’s $D$ calculates the distance between the joint rank distribution and the product of the marginal rank distributions. It detects arbitrary dependence, handles ties gracefully, and is asymptotically zero if and only if the variables are strictly independent. Furthermore, his foundational theory of U-statistics elegantly unifies the mathematical machinery for finding minimum-variance unbiased estimators. The fact that working data scientists stumble around with fragile heuristics while Hoeffding’s bulletproof tools gather dust in the 1948 archives perfectly validates your premise.

If Hoeffding is the gold standard, here is my rank-ordered list of the top 30 other unsung "wizards" of statistics and probability. They are ranked by the profundity of their insight, the "magical" utility of their tools, and the stark disparity between their brilliance and their obscurity among mainstream practitioners.

The Arch-Mages of Statistical Theory (1–5)

These figures broke the foundational paradigms of the field.

1. Charles Stein

The Wizard’s Tool: Stein’s Paradox and Stein’s Method.

Why: Stein mathematically proved the single most counter-intuitive fact in modern statistics: when estimating three or more independent means, standard averaging is mathematically "inadmissible." You can strictly lower your total error by shrinking independent estimates toward a grand mean. It birthed modern regularization (Ridge/Lasso). Furthermore, his "Stein's Method" is a dark-arts technique to bound the distance between probability distributions using differential equations, bypassing the need for characteristic functions and solving network dependencies the Central Limit Theorem cannot touch.
2. Lucien Le Cam

The Wizard’s Tool: Local Asymptotic Normality (LAN) and Contiguity.

Why: Le Cam is the final boss of asymptotic statistics. His math is notoriously impenetrable, keeping him unknown to the masses. He abstracted statistics into a metric geometry where distances are measured between entire statistical experiments. LAN allows a wizard to substitute a horrifically complex, non-normal likelihood problem with a simple Gaussian shift experiment in the asymptotic limit, making impossible proofs suddenly solvable.
3. Herbert Robbins

The Wizard’s Tool: Empirical Bayes and Stochastic Approximation.

Why: Robbins is the unsung grandfather of AI. His Robbins-Monro algorithm is the theoretical bedrock of Stochastic Gradient Descent. But his pure statistical wizardry is Empirical Bayes: he proved that if you have hundreds of parallel problems, you can use the frequentist aggregate data to mathematically construct a "prior" that shrinks and improves the estimate of every individual problem, bridging the Frequentist-Bayesian war decades early.
4. Jaroslav Hájek

The Wizard’s Tool: The Hájek Projection.

Why: A tragic genius working behind the Iron Curtain, Hájek provided the rigorous mathematical backbone for rank-based statistics. Ranks are fundamentally dependent (ranking one item 1st changes the ranks of the rest), making variance calculations nightmarish. The Hájek Projection is a mind-bending mathematical trick that perfectly projects these dependent statistics onto a sum of independent random variables, making their asymptotic analysis miraculously elegant.
5. Thomas Ferguson

The Wizard’s Tool: The Dirichlet Process.

Why: The absolute bedrock of Bayesian non-parametrics. Ferguson formulated a distribution over distributions. Instead of hard-coding the number of clusters or parameters in a model, the Dirichlet Process allows wizards to build infinite mixture models where the complexity of the model grows organically with the data.
The Architects of Hidden Geometry (6–15)

These researchers solved the great paradoxes of space, dependence, and structure.

6. Frank Hampel

The Wizard’s Tool: The Influence Function and Breakdown Point.

Why: The father of modern Robust Statistics. The Influence Function is effectively the calculus "derivative" of a statistic: it measures exactly how an infinitesimal point-mass of outlier contamination will warp your estimator. Hampel formalized how to mathematically X-ray algorithms for vulnerabilities to bad data.
7. I.J. (Isidore Jacob) Good

The Wizard’s Tool: Good-Turing Frequency Estimation.

Why: Alan Turing’s cryptanalysis partner at Bletchley Park. Good solved the "zero-frequency problem": How do you mathematically estimate the probability of encountering an event you have never seen yet? Good proved you can calculate the probability mass of the unseen by using the frequency of items you've seen exactly once. It is a paradigm-shifting tool heavily hoarded by elite NLP engineers.
8. Henry E. Daniels

The Wizard’s Tool: Saddlepoint Approximations.

Why: The Central Limit Theorem fails spectacularly in the extreme tails (predicting 1-in-10,000 rare events). Borrowing from complex contour integration in quantum physics, Daniels mapped moment-generating functions back to density functions. It yields magically accurate probabilities in the extreme tails even with a sample size of $N=3$.
9. Murray Rosenblatt

The Wizard’s Tool: The Rosenblatt Transformation.

Why: A jaw-dropping sequential conditioning transformation that maps any absolutely continuous, highly dependent multivariate distribution into a set of perfectly independent Uniform(0,1) variables on a hypercube. It is the absolute theoretical holy grail for modern Copula theory and dependency decoupling.
10. Julian Besag

The Wizard’s Tool: The Hammersley-Clifford Theorem & Pseudo-likelihood.

Why: Calculating the global joint probability of millions of connected spatial nodes (like pixels) requires impossible partition functions. Besag proved that you can rigorously reconstruct the massive global distribution purely by looking at the conditional probabilities of a node given its immediate neighbors. It is the hidden DNA of Markov Random Fields.
11. Grace Wahba

The Wizard’s Tool: The Representer Theorem and Generalized Cross-Validation (GCV).

Why: Everyone who uses a Support Vector Machine or kernel method is standing on Wahba's shoulders. She proved that an infinitely complex optimization problem (fitting a curve in an infinite-dimensional Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space) can be exactly solved in a finite-dimensional matrix spanned strictly by the training data points.
12. Peter Whittle

The Wizard’s Tool: The Whittle Likelihood.

Why: Calculating the exact likelihood of massive time-series or spatial data requires inverting giant covariance matrices, which is computationally ruinous ($O(N^3)$). Whittle bypassed the time domain and approximated the likelihood in the frequency (Fourier) domain using periodograms, performing the calculation instantly.
13. John Aitchison
The Wizard’s Tool: Compositional Data Analysis (The Log-Ratio Transform).

Why: Whenever data sums to a constant (e.g., percentages, chemical assays, market share), standard correlations are mathematically spurious because an increase in one variable forces a decrease in the others. Aitchison invented the geometric transforms (ILR/CLR) to map bounded fractions safely into infinite Euclidean space. Ignoring his work is the biggest widespread error in modern data science.

14. A.P. Dawid
The Wizard’s Tool: Prequential Analysis and Conditional Independence ($\perp\!\!\!\perp$).

Why: Dawid stripped conditional probability down to its axioms (he literally invented the $\perp\!\!\!\perp$ symbol used in all causal inference). Furthermore, his "prequential" probability evaluates models purely on their sequential ability to predict the next data point, completely bypassing the metaphysical debate over whether a "true" model even exists.

15. E.T. Jaynes
The Wizard’s Tool: The Maximum Entropy Principle (MaxEnt).

Why: Jaynes showed that statistical mechanics and Bayesian inference are the exact same math. MaxEnt solves the problem of "objective priors." It dictates that the optimal probability distribution is the one that maximizes Shannon entropy subject strictly to the constraints you actually know, ensuring you inject zero hidden assumptions into your model.

The Masters of Edge Cases and Extremes (16–25)
The experts in the tails, the skeptics, and the robust.

16. Samuel Karlin

The Wizard’s Tool: Total Positivity ($TP_2$).

Why: He explored the deep algebraic properties of probability matrices. If a distribution kernel is "totally positive," Karlin proved it mathematically guarantees the existence of optimal hypothesis tests (Monotone Likelihood Ratios) and guarantees that complex Markov Chains will converge smoothly without oscillating.

17. Arthur Dempster
The Wizard’s Tool: Dempster-Shafer Theory of Evidence.

Why: Though famous as the "D" in the EM algorithm, Dempster's solo wizardry is Epistemic Probability. In standard Bayes, total ignorance forces a 50/50 prior—but 50/50 implies you know it's a coin flip. Dempster decoupled uncertainty from ignorance, allowing probability mass to be assigned to sets to mathematically compute with pure, objective doubt.

18. Ulf Grenander
The Wizard’s Tool: Pattern Theory and The Grenander Estimator.

Why: The Grenander Estimator is a non-parametric maximum likelihood estimator for strictly decreasing density functions (isotonic regression). It allows you to fit monotonic curves beautifully based purely on shape constraints, requiring absolutely no tuning parameters or arbitrary smoothing bandwidths.

19. Paul Rosenbaum
The Wizard’s Tool: Rosenbaum Bounds for Sensitivity Analysis.

Why: In observational causal inference, unmeasured confounders are the ultimate enemy. Rosenbaum Bounds allow a wizard to mathematically calculate exactly how powerful a hidden, invisible variable would have to be to completely erase a published study's statistical significance. It is the ultimate bullshit-detector.

20. David Freedman
The Wizard’s Tool: Freedman’s Paradox.

Why: The ultimate mathematical warning against "letting the data speak." Freedman mathematically proved that if you take entirely independent, completely random noise variables and run stepwise feature selection, you will routinely generate models with highly "significant" $p$-values and falsely inflated $R^2$.

21. Jack Kiefer
The Wizard’s Tool: The General Equivalence Theorem.
Why: Statisticians usually analyze data after it's collected. Kiefer formalized Optimal Experimental Design, proving that choosing physical data points to minimize the variance of your parameter estimates ($D$-optimality) is mathematically perfectly identical to minimizing the maximum variance of your model's predictions ($G$-optimality).

22. John A. Hartigan

The Wizard’s Tool: Hartigan’s Dip Test.
Why: Today, people use histograms with arbitrary bin sizes to guess if data has multiple peaks. Hartigan's Dip Test measures the exact maximum distance between your empirical distribution and the best-fitting unimodal distribution, providing a rigorous, parameter-free $p$-value for multimodality.

23. Abe Sklar

The Wizard’s Tool: Sklar’s Theorem.
Why: Sklar proved that any multivariate joint distribution can be cleanly decoupled into its univariate marginals and a "Copula" that captures all the dependence. This allows wizards to model systems where Variable A is Gaussian, Variable B has massive fat tails, yet their exact joint dependency is perfectly preserved.

24. Jorma Rissanen

The Wizard’s Tool: Minimum Description Length (MDL).
Why: Rissanen bridged information theory and inference. MDL posits that the best statistical model is simply the one that allows you to mathematically compress the data the most. It provides a profound, objective alternative to standard $p$-values or AIC/BIC without ever assuming a "true" data-generating mechanism exists.

25. Colin Mallows

The Wizard’s Tool: Mallows Models and Mallows Distance.
Why: How do you calculate the "average" of five different people's ranked top-10 lists? Euclidean distance is meaningless. Mallows created a family of distance-based probability distributions over permutations. He also introduced the Wasserstein distance into statistics decades before the machine learning community renamed it the "Earth Mover's Distance."

The Deep Mechanics (26–30)

26. D.A.S. Fraser
The Wizard’s Tool: Tangent Exponential Models & Structural Inference.

Why: Frequentists rely on large-sample limits. Fraser married differential geometry and group theory to statistics, creating a framework that extracts miraculously exact $p$-values and confidence intervals in highly non-linear parameter spaces, completely bypassing the failures of standard likelihoods.

27. Albert Shiryaev

The Wizard’s Tool: The Shiryaev-Roberts Procedure.
Why: A Soviet wizard of stochastic calculus, Shiryaev defined the rigorous math of "when to quit." His procedure mathematically defines the optimal way to detect a sudden change-point or regime shift in a live, noisy data stream with the absolute minimum expected delay.

28. Raj Bahadur

The Wizard’s Tool: The Bahadur Representation of Quantiles.
Why: Finding a median or quantile is mathematically ugly because it requires sorting. Bahadur proved an astonishing asymptotic representation that expresses sample quantiles as an elegant sum of independent random variables, unlocking the calculus of complex robust statistics.

29. Maurice Bartlett

The Wizard’s Tool: The Bartlett Correction.
Why: The Likelihood Ratio Test relies on an approximation that breaks down in small samples. Bartlett found a magical algebraic adjustment: by multiplying the test statistic by a specific constant, the approximation error miraculously drops from $O(1/n)$ to $O(1/n^2)$, saving hypothesis testing out of thin air.

30. Emil Julius Gumbel

The Wizard’s Tool: Extreme Value Theory (The Gumbel Distribution).
Why: Normal distributions model the average; Gumbel built the rigorous mathematics for the absolute maximums and minimums. His work gives us the machinery to predict the probabilities of black swan events—100-year floods, massive market crashes, and catastrophic equipment failures—using ordinary data.
