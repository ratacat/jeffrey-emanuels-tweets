Falsifiability and fit

This thesis yields predictions that line up with the data:
•Severity tracks wartime distortion. Economies with larger WWI‑era debasement/debt (and harsher postwar realignments) faced sharper deflation and banking stress in 1930–33. Austria, Germany, and several Central/Eastern European states—each with extreme postwar monetary disorders—were early crisis points.  
•Exit from the war‑era gold straightjacket speeds recovery. The earlier a country abandoned the interwar gold rules, the quicker it turned the corner—consistent with a monetary, not trade, bottleneck.  
•Concentration + sterilization predicts global deflation. As U.S./France raised their gold shares and sterilized inflows, world prices fell; contemporary official reports (League of Nations Gold Delegation; BIS) recognized the binding constraint as gold distribution/hoarding, not “animal spirits.”

Bottom line

The Great Crash became the Great Depression because the world tried to bolt a pre‑war monetary rule onto post‑war money and debt. WWI’s financing—massive borrowing and monetary expansion across the belligerents—was the root shock. Everything that followed—the misaligned parities, reparation‑debt loops, gold hoarding and sterilization, the Fed’s whipsaw between supporting parities and choking speculation, the banking collapses—was a downstream manifestation of that original monetary debasement and debt overhang. Strip out the war’s monetary legacy, and 1929 looks far more like a painful but ordinary recession than a civilization‑bending depression. (Speculation, but consistent with the evidence above.)
