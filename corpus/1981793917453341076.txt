Thesis. The 1929 crash and the global depression that followed were the culmination of a single, system‑level imbalance created by World War I: a war‑financed explosion of money and debt that broke the pre‑1914 monetary order, forced a distorted restoration of the gold standard on misaligned parities, and left a world of fragile balance sheets dependent on short‑term dollar funding and a chronically scarce (and increasingly hoarded) monetary base. The crash was a trigger; the war’s monetary overhang was the fuse.  ￼

The causal chain, in six links
1.Wartime monetary debasement and debt. Belligerents suspended gold convertibility and financed unprecedented expenditures with a mix of borrowing and money creation. In the U.S. alone, broad money (M2) rose ~70% from 1916 to 1920 and the price level ~85%; roughly a quarter of U.S. war finance was monetary (direct + indirect), the rest overwhelmingly debt. Similar playbooks—convertibility suspensions, note issuance, and heavy borrowing—prevailed across Europe. This was textbook monetary debasement, and it was global.  ￼
2.A warped return to gold. After 1918, governments tried to “re‑legitimize” currencies by going back to gold—but on the wreckage of wartime inflation. Britain re‑pegged sterling in 1925 at its prewar parity (overvalued), locking in deflationary pressure; France stabilized later, deliberately devaluing the franc to roughly one‑fifth of its prewar gold value in 1928 (undervalued). The same war shock thus produced asymmetry: one major economy forced into chronic deflation, another into persistent external surpluses.  ￼
3.The reparations/war‑debt triangle financed by U.S. credit. Wartime debts and Versailles reparations created a payments loop: Germany paid reparations to the Allies; the Allies serviced war debts to the U.S.; and the loop was made to “work” by American loans to Germany under the Dawes (1924) and Young (1929) arrangements. That solution deepened the system’s dependence on short‑term, pro‑cyclical dollar funding and on Wall Street’s risk appetite. When those flows slowed after 1928, the loop unraveled.  ￼
4.A structural shortage of effective monetary gold. Because wartime debasement had wrecked credibility, capital sought the safest anchors. Gold increasingly pooled in the U.S. and (after its 1928 stabilization) France—both of which frequently sterilized inflows. By 1931–32, the two held an outsized share of the world’s monetary gold (over 60% by 1932), which, under a gold‑based system, meant a global shortage of liquidity everywhere else. Sterilization converted that shortage into deflation.  ￼
5.Policy choices were constrained by the war’s monetary legacy. The Fed’s 1924 and 1927 easings were used in part to prop up the restored gold system and Britain’s parity; then, in 1928–29, the Fed tightened to curb speculation. Under a gold system already starved of unsterilized reserves, that tightening propagated internationally with force. The crash was the obvious symptom; the transmission mechanism (gold) and its fragility (postwar) were the pathology.  ￼
6.Crisis became depression because balance sheets were war‑distorted. Once U.S. credit to Germany and Central Europe reversed and gold fled to the “core,” the European banking crisis of 1931 and the U.S. panics of 1931–33 were almost baked in: over‑levered sovereigns and banks, collateral values falling with deflation, and no lender of last resort compatible with gold parities. Those panics are the consequences of the wartime monetary regime and its botched normalization, not independent causes.  ￼
