I mostly agree with your characterization of Hoeffding—with one nuance: among modern practitioners he’s “famous” for Hoeffding’s inequality, but the parts you’re pointing to (dependence + nonparametrics) are absolutely under-read relative to their depth.

In his 1948 paper, Hoeffding defined a population measure of deviation from independence in terms of the joint CDF vs. the product of marginals, and derived an unbiased estimator that yields a consistent (nonparametric, rank-based) test of independence for continuous alternatives. (Wikipedia)
That dependence functional and its estimator (commonly discussed as “Hoeffding’s D / Hoeffding’s independence test”) is still a conceptual prototype for “detect any departure from independence, not just monotone correlation.” It also comes with subtleties (e.g., the continuous-distribution caveat and related modifications such as Blum–Kiefer–Rosenblatt). (Wikipedia)
Historically it also carried a “hard to compute / hard to teach” penalty (U‑statistic / rank-combinatorics), though there are now efficient implementations and modern “fast rank test” treatments. (https://t.co/HkNYe0Jd2f)

So yes: if your bar for “unsung” is “people whose work is everywhere implicitly, but whose actual ideas aren’t in most practitioners’ heads,” Hoeffding (beyond the inequality) fits extremely well.

Below is my ranked top 30 (excluding Hoeffding) under that bar. Ranking is subjective; I’m weighting: (i) conceptual payoff per page, (ii) breadth of applicability, (iii) under-recognition among working statisticians/ML practitioners.

Top 30 other “unsung heroes” of probability & statistics (ranked)

1) Lucien Le Cam

Required-reading idea: Asymptotic experiments (Le Cam distance/deficiency; contiguity; LAN) — a unifying language for “when two statistical problems are essentially the same.”
Why it matters: It lets you transfer optimality and lower bounds across problems instead of reproving everything from scratch; it’s the hidden backbone of modern asymptotic decision theory. (Project Euclid)

2) Jaroslav Hájek
Required-reading idea: the Hájek projection (and the rank-test asymptotics it powers).
Why it matters: It’s the cleanest mental model for why many complicated rank/U‑statistics behave “as if” they were sums of i.i.d. terms, yielding CLTs, efficiency statements, and local power calculations that are still underused. (Kybernetika)

3) Jean Ville

Required-reading idea: Ville’s inequality + the “test martingale/supermartingale” view of evidence.
Why it matters: This is a deep alternative foundation for sequential testing and optional stopping safety—very close in spirit to today’s e-values and anytime-valid inference, yet most people only meet it indirectly. (Probability and Finance)
4) Peter J. Huber

Required-reading idea: the contamination model and Huber’s robust M‑estimation (Huber loss as the canonical bridge between mean and median).
Why it matters: Robustness isn’t an “add-on”—it’s a different asymptotic regime. Huber’s 1964 paper is still the crispest place to learn that regime properly. (Project Euclid)

5) Raghu Raj Bahadur
Required-reading idea: Bahadur representation (e.g., for sample quantiles) + “exact slopes” / Bahadur efficiency.
Why it matters: It gives a near-linear expansion for non-smooth functionals (quantiles, ranks), which is a workhorse behind modern limit theory but rarely taught with the clarity it deserves. (Project Euclid)

6) Frank R. Hampel

Required-reading idea: the influence function (and the mindset of local robustness / gross-error sensitivity).
Why it matters: Influence functions are the microscope for estimator behavior under perturbations; they unify robust stats, semiparametrics, and even modern “sensitivity analysis” thinking. (JSTOR)
7) Ulf Grenander

Required-reading idea: the Grenander estimator (shape-constrained NPMLE for a monotone density).
Why it matters: Shape constraints are one of the few places where nonparametric estimation is simultaneously interpretable, computationally clean, and theoretically sharp—and Grenander is the prototype. (Project Euclid)

8) Jack Kiefer
Required-reading idea: Kiefer–Wolfowitz foundations for nonparametric MLE and stochastic approximation.
Why it matters: If you care about modern latent-variable likelihoods, mixtures, and nonparametric likelihood geometry, Kiefer’s work is a deep root. The 1956 paper on consistency with infinitely many nuisance parameters is a landmark, and the 1952 Kiefer–Wolfowitz stochastic approximation is foundational for derivative-free optimization. (Project Euclid)

9) Murray Rosenblatt

Required-reading idea: strong mixing (α‑mixing) as a usable dependence condition for CLTs and asymptotics.
Why it matters: Dependence is the default in time series and stochastic processes; Rosenblatt’s mixing framework is one of the main bridges from i.i.d. asymptotics to reality. (PMC)
10) Richard M. Dudley

Required-reading idea: metric entropy integrals controlling suprema/continuity of Gaussian processes (the “Dudley integral”).
Why it matters: This is one of the conceptual keystones behind empirical process theory and modern learning theory generalization bounds—yet many practitioners never see the underlying geometry. (Project Euclid)

11) Herbert Robbins
Required-reading idea: Robbins–Monro stochastic approximation (and the broader “learn by noisy feedback” paradigm).
Why it matters: This is the ancestor of a huge chunk of optimization-under-noise: root finding, online learning, bandits, and stochastic gradient ideas. Reading the original makes the core assumptions and logic very clear. (Project Euclid)

12) James Hannan

Required-reading idea: Hannan consistency (early no-regret decision-making in repeated play).
Why it matters: Modern online learning/regret minimization has “rediscovered” this line many times. Hannan’s 1957 work is one of the deep early sources that connects decision theory, games, and prediction. (Wharton Stats)
13) Gideon Schwarz

Required-reading idea: BIC (Bayesian Information Criterion) derived from asymptotic Bayes model selection.
Why it matters: Everyone uses BIC; far fewer have read the short paper that cleanly explains what it is (an asymptotic approximation to a Bayes solution) and when it’s justified. (Project Euclid)

14) I. J. Good
Required-reading idea: the Good–Turing estimator (inference about unseen mass / “frequency of frequencies”).
Why it matters: It’s a beautiful example of how much information lives in the histogram of counts, and it keeps reappearing—in language modeling, ecology, genomics, and privacy. (OUP Academic)

15) Maurice Quenouille

Required-reading idea: the jackknife as bias-reduction via leave-one-out pseudo-values.
Why it matters: Bootstrap culture largely displaced jackknife intuition, but the jackknife is still the cleanest route to understanding resampling as linearization and bias correction. (JSTOR)
16) P. H. Peskun

Required-reading idea: Peskun ordering for comparing MCMC kernels by asymptotic variance.
Why it matters: If you do MCMC, you want principled ways to say “this transition is better than that one.” Peskun’s criterion is one of the sharpest such ideas and is still under-known outside MCMC specialists. (JSTOR)

17) Emanuel Parzen
Required-reading idea: the Parzen window kernel density estimator (and early nonparametric smoothing theory).
Why it matters: KDE is ubiquitous; Parzen’s original treatment is a model of how to reason about smoothing bias/variance tradeoffs without pretending everything is parametric. (Bayes Institute)

18) Herman Wold

Required-reading idea: Wold decomposition of covariance-stationary time series into deterministic + innovation-driven components.
Why it matters: It’s the conceptual skeleton behind ARMA thinking: any “nice enough” stationary series is (in essence) an infinite MA of uncorrelated innovations plus a predictable part. (Korora)
19) Abe Sklar

Required-reading idea: Sklar’s theorem (copulas: separating marginals from dependence).
Why it matters: This is one of the few universally useful “dependence normal forms.” Practitioners often learn copulas as a bag of families; Sklar’s original insight is the real key. (MDPI)

20) Julius R. Blum
Required-reading idea: Blum–Kiefer–Rosenblatt distribution-free independence tests (and the product-measure correction to Hoeffding-style dependence functionals).
Why it matters: If you like Hoeffding’s D, BKR is the next stop: independence testing framed via the empirical distribution function with strong distribution-free properties. (Project Euclid)

21) H. O. Hirschfeld

Required-reading idea: maximal correlation (the Hirschfeld–Gebelein–Rényi concept) as a principled nonlinear dependence measure.
Why it matters: “Correlation” doesn’t have to mean linear; maximal correlation is a rigorous way to ask for the strongest correlation achievable by measurable transforms—hugely insightful for dependence, information flow, and feature extraction. (Cambridge University Press & Assessment)
22) V. V. Yurinskii

Required-reading idea: Yurinskii coupling for approximating sums of independent (or weakly dependent) vectors by Gaussian vectors with explicit error control.
Why it matters: This is one of the quiet workhorses of high-dimensional CLT/strong approximation arguments in modern asymptotic econometrics and statistics. (Cambridge University Press & Assessment)

23) János Komlós
Required-reading idea: the KMT/Hungarian embedding (strong approximation of empirical processes / random walks by Brownian objects).
Why it matters: It’s the “turbocharger” for asymptotics: not just weak convergence, but almost-sure couplings with sharp rates—unlocking nonasymptotic-looking bounds from asymptotic machinery. (Kurimskyoto)

24) Péter Major

Required-reading idea: (with Komlós & Tusnády) strong embedding techniques that make empirical process approximations brutally effective.
Why it matters: Many refined results in goodness-of-fit, quantiles, and uniform bands become straightforward once you internalize how KMT-type couplings work. (Kurimskyoto)
25) Gábor Tusnády

Required-reading idea: (with Komlós & Major) the empirical-process Brownian bridge coupling perspective.
Why it matters: It’s hard to overstate how often modern “sharp” empirical-process arguments are just KMT plus clever bookkeeping—yet few practitioners ever read the original lineage. (Kurimskyoto)

26) Volker Strassen
Required-reading idea: Strassen’s theorem on stochastic domination via couplings (monotone couplings exist iff the stochastic order holds).
Why it matters: Coupling is one of the deepest “secret weapons” in probability. Strassen’s theorem is a canonical bridge between an inequality/order statement and an explicit joint construction. (Project Euclid)

27) A. V. Skorokhod

Required-reading idea: Skorokhod representation (turn weak convergence into almost sure convergence on a richer probability space).
Why it matters: It’s the tool that turns convergence-in-law into something you can manipulate pathwise—often simplifying proofs and intuition in limit theorems and stochastic-process convergence. (Wikipedia)
28) David L. Burkholder

Required-reading idea: Burkholder–Davis–Gundy inequalities (moment control of martingales via quadratic variation).
Why it matters: If martingales are your calculus, BDG is your norm equivalence. It’s indispensable in stochastic integration, SDEs, and modern concentration for dependent data. (Project Euclid)

29) Nikolai Chentsov
Required-reading idea: Chentsov’s theorem (Fisher information metric is essentially the unique statistic-invariant Riemannian metric).
Why it matters: This is “why Fisher information is the natural geometry of parametric models,” not just a quantity. It quietly underpins information geometry and deep connections between inference and geometry. (Wikipedia)

30) Elliott W. Barankin

Required-reading idea: Barankin bounds (extremely tight lower bounds on estimator variance; more general than Cramér–Rao).
Why it matters: CRLB is often too optimistic outside regular models. Barankin’s approach is one of the most powerful correctives—rarely taught, hugely clarifying when you care about fundamental limits. (Project Euclid)
