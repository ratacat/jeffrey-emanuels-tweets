This article I wrote a few months ago is a good accessible intro to the basic ideas:

https://t.co/xMBvkYk8g2

That’s useful if you want to get into the nitty gritty of the implementation details or want to use it in your own Rust projects.

Anyway, what I want to point out here is something different, which is the way the two current frontier models (GPT-5.5 and Opus 4.8 with max thinking) approach the problem of explaining a key part of the algorithm.

I asked both models this question first:

“What’s the relationship between RaptorQ and the Galois field GF(2^8) with x^8 + x^4 + x^3 + x^2 + 1 ?”

Perhaps unsurprisingly, they both responded with a terse, highly technical response, matching the tone of the question. But then I asked both the same follow-up question:

“Can you unpack basically all of that, each part of it so you can understand the WHY and where it comes from and the intuition behind it?”

Both then gave very clear, detailed answers, which you can see below:

Claude:

https://t.co/GTXS3T0lSw

GPT:

https://t.co/HSIETDVCC5

I’m curious to hear what people think, but for my money, the Claude explanation is just much better and easier to read and understand. It sounds more human-centric somehow. 

I actually think that GPT is strictly “higher IQ” than Claude, but somehow Claude has a better internal model of how humans think and communicate.

Anyway, I highly recommend reading these. If you only read one, then read the Claude one. If it still reads like Greek to you, then add a follow-up to simplify each part of it much more and provide more background context and explanation. 

RaptorQ is one of those things that seems like magic to me no matter how much I dig into it. Like some kind of free lunch that shouldn’t exist, but somehow does.
